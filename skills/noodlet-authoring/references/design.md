# Designing for students

Noodlets are played by students — but "students" covers a wide range, from young
children to adult learners. Consider your target audience for each of the choices below.

## Feedback and scoring

- **Younger learners / low-stakes practice** — lean toward encouragement and effort
  celebration; soften or omit raw scores; use friendly language for incorrect answers.
- **Older or exam-facing learners** — honest, precise feedback is often more useful than
  cushioning; consider showing exact scores and clear right/wrong explanations.

## Retries and pacing

- **Mastery-style activities** — allow unlimited retries so learners can work until they
  get it right; remove time pressure entirely.
- **Assessment or challenge activities** — limited attempts or a time limit may be
  appropriate if that matches the pedagogical goal.

## Leaderboards and competition

- Consider whether a leaderboard motivates or discourages your audience. Competitive
  ranking suits some contexts (older students, gamified practice) but can harm
  confidence in others (younger children, mixed-ability groups).

## Interface clarity

- Regardless of age, aim for obvious affordances: clear call-to-action buttons, readable
  text sizes, and a layout that communicates what to do without requiring separate
  instructions.
- Younger or less experienced users benefit from larger touch targets and simpler layouts.

## Reporting results

Report a result when the student reaches a natural finish (see the **contract** doc) —
`submitResult` then `complete` — so the teacher sees their progress.
